About Me

I was initially drawn to science by the incredible intricacy of biological organization, intrigued by how subtle disruptions to cellular precision can lead to fatal disease. Over three and a half years at New York University Abu Dhabi, my scientific focus has been carved at the most challenging intersections of immunology, neurobiology, and cancer research. I have a deep desire to bridge feilds and understand biology through a systemic lens.

My independent thesis research centers on optimizing T-cells in tertiary lymphoid organoids to combat solid tumors. The goal: circumvent immune exhaustion in the tumor microenvironment by enhancing T-cell energetics through mitochondrial transfer from bone marrow stem cells. This work could open a new path to cellular immunotherapy.

Research

Flagship project · 2024 — Present · LIBRA Lab, NYUAD · Prof. Jeremy Teo
Independent thesis

Optimizing CD8+ T-Cells via Mitochondrial Transfer for Enhanced Anti-Tumor Activity in Tertiary Lymphoid Organoids

Solid tumors are notoriously adept at exhausting the immune cells sent to destroy them. My thesis asks a fundamental question: what if we could re-energize those T-cells before the fight begins?

I developed an optimized co-culture system pairing isolated and activated CD8+ T-cells from PBMCs with bone marrow stromal cells (BMSCs) to facilitate intercellular mitochondrial transfer. After months of full-time + part-time experimental work, extensive flow cytometry and fluorescence confocal microscopy, I demonstrated successful mitochondrial transfer at an average rate of 15%.

The next in progress: integrating the metabolically enhanced T-cells into tertiary lymphoid organoids (TLOs). These organoids, developed by differentiating ADSCs into fibroblastic reticular cell-like phenotypes using a precise cytokine cocktail, mimic the lymphoid microenvironment that organizes immune responses. When injected near tumor sites, TLOs may efficiently boost anti-tumor immune activity. I am currently validating efficacy using 3D tumor spheroid co-culture systems.

Immuno-oncology · Spring 2024 · LIBRA Lab, NYUAD · First project

Hypoxia-Driven Metabolic Reprogramming in the Tumor Microenvironment

My entry point into immuno-oncology was studying how the oxygen deprived environment inside solid tumors reshapes cancer cell metabolism. The hypoxic tumor microenvironment actively forces metabolic alterations that drive aggressive phenotypes and immune evasion.

I spent two months optimizing a 3D tumor spheroid protocol, experimenting with varying hydrogel concentrations to accurately model hypoxic conditions in vitro. I validated the model using flow cytometry, Seahorse XF metabolic analysis for real-time respirometry, and multiple rounds of fluorescence imaging. This project built the experimental foundation for what followed.

Biophysics · Sep – Dec 2023 · Lab for Soft & Cellular Matter · Prof. Gholami · First research role

Waveform Dynamics of Eukaryotic Flagella: High-Speed 3D Imaging of Chlamydomonas reinhardtii

My first research experience placed me at the founding of a brand-new laboratory. The first challenge was to figure out how to immobilize single-cell algae precisely enough to image their flagellar beating in 3D. I conducted what became my first literature review by consulting neighboring PIs, and discovered that a neighboring lab was developing a tool perfectly suited to our problem.

Using high-speed 3D microscopy, I generated and analyzed complex waveform patterns emerging from flagellar dynamics. The visuals were wonderful, but this project also showed me that I wanted research with a direct therapeutic translation.
